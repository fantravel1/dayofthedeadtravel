Before You Book a Flight

Understanding comes first. Travel planning comes second.

Day of the Dead is not a festival to attend. It is a living tradition to witness. Before you plan logistics, understand what you are stepping into.

This is a relationship between the living and the dead that has existed for thousands of years. It predates colonization, survived suppression, and continues to evolve across dozens of regions and indigenous communities throughout Mexico. It is not a single event. It is a worldview — one where death is not an ending but a continuation, where the dead return each year to be with their families, to eat their favorite foods, to hear the music they loved.

"Your role is that of a respectful witness — testigo respetuoso."

Understanding comes first, travel planning comes second. Read these pages not to plan a trip, but to understand a tradition. Once you understand, the right trip will become clear.

The Five Things You Must Know

Before you do anything else, understand these five truths.

☠ Essential Knowledge for Every Visitor

  1. This is NOT Halloween. Different origins, different meaning, different everything. Day of the Dead is rooted in indigenous Mesoamerican beliefs about death and remembrance. It is sacred, not spooky.
  2. Every region is different. Oaxaca, Michoacan, CDMX, Puebla, Yucatan — each has unique traditions, indigenous roots, and ways of honoring the dead. There is no single "Day of the Dead experience."
  3. You are a guest, not a participant (unless invited). Observe with respect. This is not your tradition to perform — it is a tradition to witness with humility and gratitude.
  4. Book early. Popular destinations sell out 6–12 months in advance. Accommodations in Oaxaca and Patzcuaro fill up fast. Plan ahead or prepare to be flexible.
  5. Learn basic Spanish. Even a few respectful phrases show you care. "Con permiso," "gracias," "lo siento" — these words demonstrate that you are here with intention, not just curiosity.

Choose Your Region

Every region honors the dead differently. Find the experience that resonates with you.

🎨

Oaxaca

Best for: Art lovers, community celebrations. Vibrant comparsas, sand tapestries (tapetes de arena), elaborate public altars. Mexico's most popular Day of the Dead destination — colorful, communal, and deeply artistic.

Explore Oaxaca →

🕯

Michoacan

Best for: Sacred intimacy. All-night candlelit vigils on the islands of Lake Patzcuaro. Purepecha indigenous traditions preserved for centuries. Mexico's most profound Day of the Dead experience.

Explore Michoacan →

🏛

Ciudad de Mexico (CDMX)

Best for: Modern + ancient blend. Grand parade, mega ofrendas, Aztec roots. The capital city's scale and cultural depth offer a sweeping experience of how tradition meets contemporary Mexico.

Explore CDMX →

🍲

Puebla

Best for: Food lovers. Home altars, mole, family-centered tradition. Intimate, domestic, deeply food-centered. Puebla's Day of the Dead is experienced at the kitchen table as much as in the cemetery.

Explore Puebla →

🌴

Yucatan & Hanal Pixan

Best for: Maya culture. Hanal Pixan — an entirely different tradition with Maya cosmology, mucbipollo, and distinct ceremonies. If you want to understand how varied Mexico's death traditions truly are, start here.

How to Prepare

A timeline from planning to arrival. Start early, arrive ready.

6–12 Months Before

Choose & Book

Choose your region. Book accommodations and flights — popular destinations like Oaxaca and Patzcuaro sell out far in advance. Start learning about the specific traditions of your chosen region. Read, watch documentaries, listen to the music.

3–6 Months Before

Plan & Learn

Book local guides — a knowledgeable local guide transforms your experience from tourism to understanding. Research specific cemetery and altar locations. Begin learning Spanish phrases: greetings, thank-yous, respectful requests.

1–3 Months Before

Prepare & Pack

Plan your day-by-day itinerary. Prepare your pack list: warm layers for cool November nights, comfortable walking shoes for cobblestone streets and cemetery paths, dark or muted clothing for respectful cemetery visits. Purchase any needed travel insurance.

1 Week Before

Final Preparations

Review etiquette guides one more time. Download offline maps for your region. Exchange currency — many smaller towns and markets are cash-only. Confirm all bookings: flights, hotels, guides, transportation.

Upon Arrival

Breathe & Acclimate

Breathe. Acclimate. Visit markets. Watch preparations unfold. Let the tradition come to you. Do not rush to the cemetery or the parade. Spend your first hours walking, watching, smelling the copal and the marigolds. The tradition will find you.

Essential Etiquette

Respect is not optional. It is the foundation of your entire experience.

✿ Quick Etiquette Guide

  • Dress modestly. Dark colors for cemeteries. This is not a costume event — it is a sacred observance. Dress as you would for a memorial, not a party.
  • Ask before photographing, especially at cemeteries. Families are grieving and remembering. A camera in the face during a private moment is an intrusion, not documentation.
  • Never touch altars or offerings. Every item on an ofrenda has been placed with intention for a specific person who has died. It is not a display for you — it is a gift for them.
  • Move quietly in sacred spaces. Cemeteries during Day of the Dead are places of profound intimacy. Lower your voice. Slow your pace. Be present, not performative.
  • Support local communities. Buy from artisans. Eat at family restaurants. Hire local guides. Your economic impact should benefit the people whose traditions you are witnessing.
